 🛡️ Warranty Policies
Manufacturer Warranty Rules
Most new vehicles come with a standard manufacturer warranty that covers defects in materials or workmanship. This warranty is provided directly by the manufacturer and varies by brand, but typically includes:
A Basic Limited Warranty (3 years / 36,000 miles)
A Powertrain Warranty (5 years / 60,000 miles)
Corrosion Perforation Coverage (5–7 years)
Roadside Assistance (varies by brand)
Manufacturer warranties apply only to the original vehicle and are non-transferable unless stated otherwise. The customer must follow the recommended maintenance schedule to keep the warranty valid.
Terms Extended warranties, also known as vehicle service contracts, offer coverage beyond the standard manufacturer warranty. These plans are optional and can be purchased at the time of sale or before the factory warranty expires. Coverage varies by plan but generally includes:
Mechanical breakdown protection
Electrical system repairs
Air conditioning and heating components
Technology systems (infotainment, sensors, cameras)
Optional roadside assistance and rental reimbursement
Extended warranty contracts are subject to deductible requirements and specific terms regarding covered vs. non-covered repairs.
Powertrain Coverage Details
Powertrain warranties cover the essential components that make the vehicle move. This includes:
Engine
Transmission
Driveshafts
Transfer case
Differentials
Axles
Powertrain coverage applies only to damage caused by normal use and manufacturing defects. Wear-and-tear items (brakes, tires, belts, filters) are not covered.
Basic Bumper-to-Bumper Warranty
A bumper-to-bumper warranty covers almost all components of a new vehicle except regular maintenance items. Typical coverage includes:
Electronics and sensors
Air conditioning
Steering components
Suspension
Interior systems
Safety systems (airbags, ABS modules)
Items not covered:
Brake pads
Tires
Filters
Wiper blades
Regular maintenance services
Coverage lasts for a limited period, usually 3 years or 36,000 miles
Return Window
Vehicles may be eligible for return or exchange within 72 hours or 150 miles, whichever comes first, depending on dealership policy. The vehicle must be in the same condition as when purchased and free from new damage or excessive wear.
Cancellation Policy

Financing is denied

The dealership explicitly provides a cancellation option
For service contracts, extended warranties, and protection plans, customers may cancel within 30 days for a full refund, provided no claims have been made.

2. Vehicle Safety Systems
Modern vehicles are equipped with a suite of active and passive safety technologies designed to prevent accidents and protect occupants.
2.1. Braking & Stability
● Anti-lock Braking System (ABS): Prevents wheel lockup during panic braking, allowing the driver to maintain steering control while braking heavily.
● Traction Control System (TCS): Detects wheel slip during acceleration and reduces engine power or applies brakes to individual wheels to regain traction.
2.2. Occupant Protection
● Airbags:
○ Front Airbags: Deploy from the steering wheel and dashboard to protect the head and chest in frontal collisions.
○ Side Airbags: Deploy from seat bolsters to protect the torso in side-impact collisions.
○ Curtain Airbags: Deploy from the roofline to cover windows, protecting heads and preventing ejection during rollovers.
2.3. Advanced Driver Assistance Systems (ADAS)
● Lane Departure Warning (LDW): Uses cameras to detect lane markings. Alerts the driver via sound or vibration if the vehicle drifts without a turn signal.
● Forward Collision Warning (FCW): Uses radar/cameras to monitor the distance to the vehicle ahead and alerts the driver of an impending impact.
● Automatic Emergency Braking (AEB): Works with FCW to automatically apply brakes if the driver does not react in time to a potential collision.
● Blind-Spot Monitoring (BSM): Uses radar sensors in the rear bumper to detect vehicles in the driver's blind zones, illuminating a light on the side mirrors.
● Rearview Cameras: A mandatory safety feature providing a video feed of the area behind the vehicle when in Reverse.
● Tire Pressure Monitoring System (TPMS): Sensors inside the tire valve stems monitor air pressure and alert the driver if pressure drops below safe levels.
3. Maintenance Schedules & Intervals
Adhering to the manufacturer's maintenance schedule is critical for vehicle reliability and maintaining warranty validity.
3.1. Routine Maintenance Intervals
● Oil Change: typically required every 5,000 to 7,500 miles. Modern vehicles with synthetic oil may extend to 10,000 miles, while severe driving conditions (stop-and-go traffic, towing) shorten this interval.
● Tire Rotation: Recommended every 6,000 miles (or every oil change) to ensure tires wear evenly, extending their lifespan and maintaining traction.
● Brake Inspection: Recommended every 10,000 miles. Technicians measure brake pad thickness (in millimeters) and inspect rotors for warping.
● Air Filters:
○ Engine Air Filter: Every 15,000 to 30,000 miles. A clogged filter reduces fuel economy and performance.
○ Cabin Air Filter: Every 15,000 miles. Filters dust, pollen, and odors from entering the HVAC system.
● Fluids: Coolant, transmission fluid, and brake fluid intervals vary by manufacturer (often 30k, 60k, or 100k miles).
4. Dashboard Warning Light Meanings
Warning lights are color-coded: Red indicates a critical issue requiring immediate attention; Yellow/Amber indicates a system needs checking soon; Green/Blue indicates a system is active.
● Check Engine Light (CEL): A yellow engine icon. A steady light indicates an emissions or sensor issue (safe to drive but check soon). A flashing light indicates a severe misfire that can damage the catalytic converter; stop driving immediately.
● Battery Light: A red battery icon. Indicates the charging system (alternator) is failing and the vehicle is running solely on battery power. The car will shut down soon.
● Oil Pressure Warning: A red oil can icon. Indicates oil pressure is dangerously low. The engine must be turned off immediately to prevent catastrophic seizure.
● ABS Light: A yellow circle with "ABS." The Anti-lock Braking System has a fault. Standard brakes will still function, but anti-lock safety features are disabled.
● Tire Pressure Light: A yellow horseshoe with an exclamation point. Indicates one or more tires are significantly underinflated (usually by 25% or more).
● Airbag/SRS Light: A yellow person with a circle. Indicates a fault in the Supplemental Restraint System. Airbags may not deploy in an accident.
● Coolant Temperature Warning: A red thermometer in liquid. The engine is overheating. Pull over immediately to let the engine cool and prevent warping the cylinder head.

Dealership Legal, Promotional & Sales Knowledge Base
9. Legal & Administrative Document Summaries
9.1. Purchase Agreement Summary
The Purchase Agreement (often referred to as the "Buyer's Order") is the definitive, legally binding contract outlining the terms of the vehicle sale. It ensures transparency regarding pricing, vehicle condition, and financial responsibilities.
Key Components:
● Vehicle Identification: Must include the Year, Make, Model, VIN (Vehicle Identification Number), and current odometer mileage.
● Price Breakdown:
○ Base Price: The agreed-upon selling price of the vehicle.
○ Add-ons: Costs for accessories, extended warranties, or protection packages.
○ Fees: Dealer documentation fees ("Doc Fee"), state sales tax, and DMV title/registration fees.
● Trade-In Details: Lists the appraised value of the customer's trade-in, the payoff amount for any existing lien, and the resulting net equity applied to the new deal.
● Payment Method: Specifies if the deal is a Cash transaction, Outside Financing, or Dealership-arranged financing.
● Warranty Disclosure: Clearly states if the vehicle is sold "As-Is" or with a remaining Manufacturer/Dealer warranty.
● Buyer’s Acknowledgments: Signatures confirming the buyer has reviewed the Odometer Disclosure Statement and the vehicle history report.
Legal Status: The agreement becomes legally binding upon signature by both the buyer (and co-buyer, if applicable) and an authorized dealership representative. It is required before the vehicle can be delivered.
9.2. Lease Agreement Summary
A Lease Agreement differs from a purchase contract as it outlines the terms for using the vehicle for a specific duration rather than owning it.
Typical Components:
● Lease Term: The duration of the contract, typically ranging from 24 to 48 months.
● Mileage Allowance: The maximum miles permitted per year (e.g., 10,000, 12,000, or 15,000 miles).
1. Excess Mileage Fee: A per-mile charge (e.g., $0.15–$0.25) applied at lease-end if limits are exceeded.
● Monthly Payment Breakdown:
1. Depreciation Fee: The cost of the vehicle's lost value over the lease term.
2. Rent Charge: Effectively the interest rate (Money Factor) for borrowing the asset.
3. Taxes: Sales tax is often applied to the monthly payment rather than the total price (state dependent).
● Upfront Costs ("Due at Signing"): Capitalized cost reduction (down payment), first month's payment, security deposit, and bank acquisition fees.
● End-of-Lease Options:
1. Return: Hand in the keys and pay any disposition fees.
2. Purchase: Buy the vehicle for the "Residual Value" stated in the contract.
3. Lease New: Trade in the lease for a new model.
● Wear & Tear Guidelines: Defines "excessive" damage (e.g., tires below 1/8th inch tread, scratches larger than a credit card) that will incur fees.
9.3. Arbitration Policy Overview
The Arbitration Policy is a dispute resolution agreement. By signing, the customer agrees to resolve legal disagreements outside of the public court system.
General Structure:
● Purpose: To resolve disputes faster and more cost-effectively than litigation.
● Scope: Covers disagreements regarding the purchase agreement, financing terms, warranty issues, or service quality.
● The Process:
1. Written Notice: The aggrieved party must submit a formal notice of dispute.
2. Internal Review: Dealership management attempts to resolve the issue directly.
3. Arbitration Hearing: If unresolved, a neutral, third-party arbitrator reviews evidence and makes a decision.
● Binding Decision: The arbitrator’s ruling is generally final and legally binding.
● Exclusions: Typically excludes criminal matters, small claims court actions (in some states), or issues non-arbitrable by state law.
9.4. Insurance Requirements Summary
Proof of insurance is a mandatory requirement for vehicle delivery to ensure liability and asset protection.
General Requirements:
● Policy Holder: The insurance policy must be active and in the name of the primary buyer or co-buyer.
● State Minimums: All drivers must meet state liability limits for bodily injury and property damage.
● Financed/Leased Vehicles: Lenders require "Full Coverage" to protect their asset.
○ Collision: Covers damage to the vehicle from an accident.
○ Comprehensive: Covers non-collision damage (theft, fire, hail).
○ Deductibles: Lenders often cap deductibles at $500 or $1,000.
● Verification: An insurance binder or valid ID card must be physically or digitally presented before the vehicle leaves the lot.
